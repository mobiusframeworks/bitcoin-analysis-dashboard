Lead-Lag Relationship Analysis

ðŸ”¬ What is Lead-Lag Analysis?
Lead-lag analysis identifies which economic indicators precede movements in Bitcoin price. If an indicator "leads" Bitcoin by X days, changes in that indicator can potentially signal future Bitcoin price movements.

âš ï¸ Critical Limitations of Lead-Lag Analysis:
  • Spurious Correlations: Historical correlations may be coincidental, not causal
  • Non-Stationary Relationships: Lead times can change as markets evolve
  • Regime Shifts: What worked in 2015-2020 may not work in 2024-2025
  • Multiple Variables: Bitcoin price is influenced by dozens of factors simultaneously
  • Sample Size: Only 3-4 complete Bitcoin cycles available for analysis (n<10)

How to Interpret These Results:

These lead-lag relationships should be viewed as one input among many in a comprehensive analysis framework. They describe historical patterns but do not guarantee future predictability. The strongest confidence (75%) is still only moderate certainty - far from deterministic prediction.

Cointegration Testing

ðŸ”¬ What is Cointegration?
Cointegration tests whether two time series have a long-term equilibrium relationship - meaning they tend to move together over time and any divergence is temporary (mean-reverting). If Bitcoin and M2 were cointegrated, large deviations from their historical relationship would eventually correct.

Result: M2 and Bitcoin are NOT cointegrated (Engle-Granger test p-value > 0.05).

Interpretation: While M2 and Bitcoin show strong correlation, they do not have a mean-reverting long-term equilibrium. This means:

  • Divergences between M2 and Bitcoin may persist indefinitely
  • No "rubber band" effect pulling them back together
  • The relationship could break down entirely in different market regimes
  • Statistical arbitrage strategies based on mean reversion would not be valid

Analytical Methods

1. Technical Indicators
  • Moving Averages (MA): Simple averages of closing price over N days (20, 50, 200)
  • Bollinger Bands: MA Â± 2 standard deviations, indicating volatility and overbought/oversold conditions
  • Fibonacci Retracements: Key levels (23.6%, 38.2%, 50%, 61.8%, 78.6%) calculated from recent high/low range
  • Support/Resistance: 365-day minimum and maximum prices
2. Correlation Analysis
  • Pearson Correlation (r): Measures linear relationship between two variables (-1 to +1)
  • Lead-Lag Testing: Shift one variable by X days and test correlation to find optimal lead period
  • Interpretation: |r| > 0.7 = strong, 0.4-0.7 = moderate, < 0.4 = weak
3. Cointegration Testing
  • Engle-Granger Test: Tests for long-term equilibrium between two time series
  • Null Hypothesis: No cointegration exists (series can drift apart indefinitely)
  • Threshold: p-value < 0.05 rejects null (cointegration detected)
  • Limitation: Assumes linear relationship and constant parameters over time
4. Machine Learning (Feature Importance)
  • Models Tested: Ridge Regression, Lasso, Random Forest, Gradient Boosting
  • Validation: TimeSeriesSplit (prevents look-ahead bias)
  • Feature Selection: Permutation importance with statistical significance testing
  • Overfitting Prevention: Removed same-day OHLC to predict same-day close (data leakage)
